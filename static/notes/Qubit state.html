Qubit state

Qubit state is a two dimensional vector representing the state space between 0 and 1. This can be denoted as follows:

\( |0\rangle := \begin{bmatrix}1\\0\end{bmatrix} \)

This is a computational basis state.

The \( |0\rangle \) notation is known as a ket and simply stands for a vector.

Just how classical bits are represented as 0 and 1, there is a \( |1\rangle \) counterpart to \( |0\rangle \).

\( |1\rangle := \begin{bmatrix}0\\1\end{bmatrix} \)

Superposition is not what I assumed, that is a qubit's ability to be in two states at once. Instead, it is a term that means the state is a linear combination of \( |0\rangle \) and \( |1\rangle \). that is they are the addition of vectors which are multiplied by scalar values.

So \( 0.6|0\rangle + 0.8|1\rangle \) is a superposition of \( |0\rangle \) and \(|1\rangle \).

The constraint of qubits is the sum of the squared scalars (or amplitudes) must equal one. As in \( 0.6^2 + 0.8^2 = 1 \). This is called a normalization constraint.
